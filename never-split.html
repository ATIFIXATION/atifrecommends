Chapter One

The New
Rules

Negotiation isn't war. It's listening.

You are not a calculator. Neither is the person across from you.   Logic-first fails. Because humans aren't logical. They are emotional, irrational, driven by things they cannot name.   Best negotiators don't argue. They simply listen.

Negotiation is not a battle of logic.
It is a process of understanding
another person's reality — and gently reshaping it.

"

Tactical Empathy is not agreeing with someone. It's understanding them well enough to move them.

02

Chapter Two

Be a
Mirror

Repeat. Pause. Let silence do the work.

Assumptions blind you. Hypotheses guide you.   Go in ready to be wrong. Ready to discover something you never expected.   The mirror technique: Repeat their last three words. Wait. Say nothing. They will fill the silence. They always do.

Assumptions blind you.
Hypotheses guide you.
Enter every negotiation ready to discover you were wrong about something important.

The Late-Night FM DJ Voice. Slow. Warm. Descending. Not cheerful. Not cold. Just: calm.
03

Chapter Three

Don't Feel
Their Pain.
Label It.

Name the emotion. Watch it shrink.

Six hours. A closed door. Three armed men on the other side.   Voss didn't demand. He didn't threaten. He just named what they felt.   The curtain moved. The door opened.

Emotions are not the obstacle to negotiation.
They are the terrain.
Don't go around them. Move through them — by naming them.

How to Label

1
Start with "It seems like..." Never "I think" or "I feel" — keep it about them, not you.
2
Name the emotion precisely eg: "It seems like you're not feeling well."
3
Wait. Say nothing else. The label does the work. You don't need to explain it.

"It seems like you're not feeling well."
Just name the emotion.

04

Chapter Four

Beware "Yes."
Master "No."

No is not the end. It's the beginning.

"Yes" can mean three things. Commitment. Confirmation. Counterfeit. Two out of three are useless.   "No" always means something. It marks a boundary. It starts an honest conversation.   Nobody compromises that often. Nobody wants to feel that softly pressured.

"No" is not rejection.
It's information.
The person who just said no now feels safe enough to engage honestly.

"

Never split the difference. Never compromise. Compromise often produces outcomes that satisfy nobody.

05

Chapter Five

Trigger the
Two Words

That's right — not you're right.

"That's right." Two words. The breakthrough in any negotiation.   It means: you've been seen. Not just heard. Seen.   "You're right" means: stop talking. "That's right" means: you understand me.   The difference is everything.

Trigger the two words that immediately transform any negotiation:
"That's right."
It opens the door to real influence.

Chapter Six

Bend
Their Reality

Make them feel what they'll lose.

$150,000 or your aunt dies. The obvious move: split it. Voss says: no.   Compromise is a principle. And principles are dangerous.   Don't tell them what they'll gain. Make them feel what they'll lose.

A good deal is not the midpoint.
It is the outcome that addresses
what each side actually needs.

07

Chapter Seven

Create the
Illusion of Control

Don't force agreement. Ask questions.

The person who gives the other side the illusion of control actually holds all of it.   Ask "How" and "What." Never "Why" — it sounds accusatory.   "How am I supposed to do that?" "What about this matters most to you?"   Let them solve the problem. When people arrive at a conclusion themselves, they believe it far more deeply.

Domination produces resistance.
Collaboration — even simulated collaboration —
produces results.

08

Chapter Eight

Guarantee
Execution

A yes that doesn't get done is not a yes.

Getting to yes is not enough. Getting to a yes that holds — that's the job.   Find the other decision-makers. Bring them along. A deal is only as good as its execution.   7% — words. 38% — tone. 55% — body language. When these don't align: it's not agreement. It's someone waiting for you to leave.

There are two decision-makers in every room.
Watch the pronouns.
"We" and "they" = others hold the power.
"I" = you're talking to the right person.

Rule of Three

1
They say yes.First commitment. Not enough on its own.
2
You summarise. They confirm: "That's right."Emotional buy-in. Now it's real.
3
Ask: "How will we know this is working?"They describe the outcome in their own words. Impossible to fake three times.
Also — find the other decision-makers. Guarantee execution means: kind other decision-makers being brought along.
09

Chapter Nine

Bargain
Hard

Hard doesn't mean cold. It means clear.

Bargain hard. Don't be aggressive. Be apologetically firm.   "This is so embarrassing. I simply can't."   That's the tone. Clarity wrapped in warmth.

Three Types of Negotiators

Accommodator

Relationship First

Hates conflict. Values harmony above the deal. Needs to know that saying no won't end the relationship.

Assertive

Time is Money

Loves directness. Needs to feel heard before they can listen. Mistake them for hostile — and you've lost them.

Analyst

Methodical, No Surprises

Needs time, data, space. Hates being rushed. Logic first, then emotion — never the reverse.

Know your own type.
Then learn to identify theirs.
Misidentifying the type across the table is the most common reason negotiations stall.

Chapter Ten

Find the
Black Swan

There are some hidden major objections of any negotiation.

Every negotiation has unknown unknowns. Pieces of information hidden deep in the other party's fears.   They are not revealed by direct questioning. They emerge from listening. Patiently. Over time.   The Black Swan is always there. The question is: are you still paying attention when it surfaces?

Black Swans are the hidden leverage
that transforms a stalled negotiation
into a resolution. Find them.

"

Every extra minute of listening is another chance to find the thing that changes everything.
